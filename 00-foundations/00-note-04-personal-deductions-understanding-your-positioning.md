# Understanding Your Positioning

**Module:** 00 · Foundations  
**Source:** Personal deductions  
**Date:** 2026-05-15

---

## Summary

Positioning is not something you stumble into — it is something you deliberately work out by asking the right questions. Before you market anything, you need to know exactly where you stand in the market: what problem you solve, for whom, why it matters, what already exists, and what makes your solution distinctly yours. These questions are the diagnostic framework for arriving at a clear, defensible position.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Positioning is the result of answering specific questions** — not a feeling or a vibe
- **It starts with the problem, not the product** — the market's need comes before your solution
- **Differentiation is non-negotiable** — if you cannot answer *how are you different*, you do not yet have a position
- **Packaging closes the loop** — features and benefits are how positioning becomes an offer the market can actually receive

---

## Details & Deep Dives

### The Positioning Framework — 7 Questions

These questions must be answered honestly and in order before any marketing strategy is built. They move from market awareness inward to offer design.

---

**1. What problems do you want to solve?**

Start here — not with your product, not with your brand aesthetic, not with which platform to post on. The problem is the foundation everything else is built on. Be specific. *"I help people make money online"* is not a problem statement. *"I help new digital creators build a monetisation strategy before they burn out from posting for free"* is.

---

**2. Who normally faces those kinds of challenges?**

Define the human being on the other side of the problem. This is your target market — not a demographic spreadsheet, but a real person with a specific situation, specific frustrations, and specific desires. The more precisely you can describe them, the more precisely you can speak to them.

---

**3. Why does solving the problem for them matter?**

This is the *stakes* question. What happens if the problem goes unsolved? What does it cost them — financially, emotionally, practically? And what becomes possible when it *is* solved? Answering this deepens your understanding of the transformation you are facilitating and sharpens the urgency in your messaging.

---

**4. What solutions are already available in the market?**

You are not operating in a vacuum. Research what already exists — direct competitors solving the same problem, indirect competitors solving it differently, and the default option of doing nothing. Understanding the existing landscape tells you what the market already accepts, what it is tired of, and where the gaps are.

---

**5. What solutions do you bring to the market?**

Now — and only now — define your offer. What is your specific answer to the problem? This is where your knowledge, skills, experience, methodology, and perspective become a product, service, or resource. Be concrete.

---

**6. How do your solutions differ from the rest of the market?**

This is your **differentiation** — the most critical positioning question. If you cannot answer this clearly, you do not yet have a position. You have a product in a crowd. Differentiation can live in:

- *Methodology* — a unique approach or framework
- *Audience specificity* — serving a niche others overlook
- *Perspective* — a worldview or philosophy that shapes everything you do
- *Packaging* — how the solution is delivered or experienced
- *Access* — price point, format, or availability
- *Identity* — who you are and the trust that comes with it

---

**7. How will you package the solution — what are the features and benefits?**

Positioning becomes an offer through packaging. This is where you decide:
- What form does the solution take? (course, service, product, community, tool)
- What is included? → **Features** — what it has and does
- What does the customer gain? → **Benefits** — the outcome and transformation
- How is it priced, delivered, and accessed?

Packaging is the bridge between your positioning and the market's ability to receive it.

---

### The Full Framework at a Glance

| # | Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What problems do you want to solve? | Your focus and purpose |
| 2 | Who faces those challenges? | Your target market |
| 3 | Why does solving it matter? | The stakes — urgency and transformation |
| 4 | What solutions already exist? | The competitive landscape and gaps |
| 5 | What solutions do you bring? | Your offer |
| 6 | How do you differ? | Your differentiation and unique position |
| 7 | How will you package it? | Features, benefits, and offer design |

## Examples

- A creator who teaches productivity **but specifically for African women juggling multiple income streams** has answered questions 1–3 with precision — and that specificity alone is a differentiator (question 6)
- Two people selling social media marketing courses: one teaches general strategy, one teaches specifically for introverts who hate being on camera — same problem, different positioning, different market, different differentiation

## My Questions

- How often should positioning be revisited as a business or personal brand evolves?
- Can you hold more than one position in the market, or does that dilute everything?

---

## Actionable Takeaways

- Work through all 7 questions for your own brand or future course — in order, without skipping
- Do not move to marketing tactics until questions 1–6 are answered with specificity
- Return to this framework whenever something in your marketing feels unclear or isn't converting — the problem is almost always upstream in the positioning

## Glossary Additions

| Term | Quick Definition |
|---|---|
| Positioning | The deliberate decision about what problem you solve, for whom, and how you differ from alternatives in the market |
| Differentiation | The specific ways your solution stands apart from existing alternatives |
| Target Market | The specific group of people whose problem you are positioned to solve |
| Offer | The packaged form of your solution — what the customer actually buys |
| Offer Design | The process of deciding the format, features, benefits, price, and delivery of your solution |
| Competitive Landscape | The full picture of existing solutions available to your target market |
